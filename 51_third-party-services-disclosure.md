# Third-Party Services Disclosure

This instrument formalizes the rules for Third-Party Services Disclosure and aligns them with the actual behavior of the application. Master QR is a camera-first Android application that scans QR codes, generates QR codes, stores scan history locally in Room, supports language switching, provides share/export flows, integrates advertising and analytics components, and is distributed across mobile app stores with platform-specific compliance requirements. The document is written in advanced legal English so it can serve as a publication-ready internal policy, a store-review support artifact, and a future contract annex. It is intentionally precise about scope, permissions, data categories, and release governance, so the text can survive scrutiny across multiple jurisdictions and app ecosystems.

Applicability to Master QR: This policy identifies the third-party services that may operate in connection with the application, including ad networks, analytics providers, crash reporting, consent tooling, or cloud services used for configuration and distribution. In a QR application, the disclosure should highlight that the scanner and generator are core native features, while external services support monetization or operational telemetry. The text should also clarify that third parties are not permitted to expand processing beyond the documented purpose, and that users should be able to understand which data categories are shared and why. The policy must therefore distinguish between what the application actually does today and what it merely might do in a future roadmap. In the current codebase, that means recognizing camera-based scanning, QR generation, local history management, language preferences, optional exporting, advertising or analytics integrations, and the absence of broad account-based processing.

Operational requirements: the developer should document purpose limitation, legal basis where relevant, retention limits, access restrictions, vendor controls, user notice requirements, and escalation paths for exceptions. If a feature is not present, the document should not pretend that it is; instead it should state that the control is reserved for future expansion. That approach is especially valuable for store review, compliance review, and internal audit preparation.

The policy should be revisited whenever the app adds new screens, new permissions, or new processing purposes.
