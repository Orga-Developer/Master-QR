# Data Sharing Disclosure

The purpose of this paper is to define a disciplined and reviewable framework for Data Sharing Disclosure in the context of Master QR. Master QR is a camera-first Android application that scans QR codes, generates QR codes, stores scan history locally in Room, supports language switching, provides share/export flows, integrates advertising and analytics components, and is distributed across mobile app stores with platform-specific compliance requirements. The document is written in advanced legal English so it can serve as a publication-ready internal policy, a store-review support artifact, and a future contract annex. It is intentionally precise about scope, permissions, data categories, and release governance, so the text can survive scrutiny across multiple jurisdictions and app ecosystems.

Applicability to Master QR: This disclosure lists which entities may receive data from the application and under what conditions. For Master QR, the principal sharing channels may include ad providers, analytics services, cloud or consent vendors, and platform operators involved in app distribution. The notice should explain the data categories shared, whether sharing is essential or optional, and whether the transfer is deterministic, event-driven, or consent-based. Users should be able to understand that scan history is principally local, while external sharing is limited to documented service purposes. The policy must therefore distinguish between what the application actually does today and what it merely might do in a future roadmap. In the current codebase, that means recognizing camera-based scanning, QR generation, local history management, language preferences, optional exporting, advertising or analytics integrations, and the absence of broad account-based processing.

Operational requirements: the developer should document purpose limitation, legal basis where relevant, retention limits, access restrictions, vendor controls, user notice requirements, and escalation paths for exceptions. If a feature is not present, the document should not pretend that it is; instead it should state that the control is reserved for future expansion. That approach is especially valuable for store review, compliance review, and internal audit preparation.

Compliance evidence, version history, and approval records should be retained so the policy can be audited against the shipped build.
