# Law Enforcement Request Policy

The purpose of this paper is to define a disciplined and reviewable framework for Law Enforcement Request Policy in the context of Master QR. Master QR is a camera-first Android application that scans QR codes, generates QR codes, stores scan history locally in Room, supports language switching, provides share/export flows, integrates advertising and analytics components, and is distributed across mobile app stores with platform-specific compliance requirements. The document is written in advanced legal English so it can serve as a publication-ready internal policy, a store-review support artifact, and a future contract annex. It is intentionally precise about scope, permissions, data categories, and release governance, so the text can survive scrutiny across multiple jurisdictions and app ecosystems.

Applicability to Master QR: This policy formalizes the workflow for validating and responding to law enforcement requests. It should identify intake channels, documentation requirements, authentication standards, emergency disclosure rules, preservation holds, and escalation to counsel or compliance owners. Because Master QR does not appear to maintain a rich account system, the policy should make clear that any production is bounded by what the platform or device context actually holds. The goal is not obstruction; it is disciplined, legally reviewable disclosure with a traceable record. The policy must therefore distinguish between what the application actually does today and what it merely might do in a future roadmap. In the current codebase, that means recognizing camera-based scanning, QR generation, local history management, language preferences, optional exporting, advertising or analytics integrations, and the absence of broad account-based processing.

Operational requirements: the developer should document purpose limitation, legal basis where relevant, retention limits, access restrictions, vendor controls, user notice requirements, and escalation paths for exceptions. If a feature is not present, the document should not pretend that it is; instead it should state that the control is reserved for future expansion. That approach is especially valuable for store review, compliance review, and internal audit preparation.
