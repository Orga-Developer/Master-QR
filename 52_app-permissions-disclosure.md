# App Permissions Disclosure

This document sets out the governing standard for App Permissions Disclosure within the legal architecture of Master QR. Master QR is a camera-first Android application that scans QR codes, generates QR codes, stores scan history locally in Room, supports language switching, provides share/export flows, integrates advertising and analytics components, and is distributed across mobile app stores with platform-specific compliance requirements. The document is written in advanced legal English so it can serve as a publication-ready internal policy, a store-review support artifact, and a future contract annex. It is intentionally precise about scope, permissions, data categories, and release governance, so the text can survive scrutiny across multiple jurisdictions and app ecosystems.

Applicability to Master QR: This disclosure explains each runtime or install-time permission requested by Master QR and ties every request to a concrete feature. The current build requests camera access for live scanning; it should not imply a broader need for storage, contacts, microphone, or location permissions unless those capabilities are genuinely introduced. The policy must describe the user experience when a permission is denied, the fallback behavior, and the principle of least privilege. A permission disclosure of this quality reduces review risk and demonstrates that the application was designed to minimize data exposure from the outset. The policy must therefore distinguish between what the application actually does today and what it merely might do in a future roadmap. In the current codebase, that means recognizing camera-based scanning, QR generation, local history management, language preferences, optional exporting, advertising or analytics integrations, and the absence of broad account-based processing.

Operational requirements: the developer should document purpose limitation, legal basis where relevant, retention limits, access restrictions, vendor controls, user notice requirements, and escalation paths for exceptions. If a feature is not present, the document should not pretend that it is; instead it should state that the control is reserved for future expansion. That approach is especially valuable for store review, compliance review, and internal audit preparation.
