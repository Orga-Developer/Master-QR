# Advertising Disclosure Policy

This document sets out the governing standard for Advertising Disclosure Policy within the legal architecture of Master QR. Master QR is a camera-first Android application that scans QR codes, generates QR codes, stores scan history locally in Room, supports language switching, provides share/export flows, integrates advertising and analytics components, and is distributed across mobile app stores with platform-specific compliance requirements. The document is written in advanced legal English so it can serve as a publication-ready internal policy, a store-review support artifact, and a future contract annex. It is intentionally precise about scope, permissions, data categories, and release governance, so the text can survive scrutiny across multiple jurisdictions and app ecosystems.

Applicability to Master QR: This document states, in plain legal and technical language, how advertisements appear within the application, what providers may serve them, and whether ad content can be personalized based on device or contextual signals. For Master QR, the disclosure should be linked to consent management, because advertising and analytics components can have privacy consequences even when the app itself remains otherwise lightweight. The policy should explain that advertising supports free access to the main scanner and generator features, while also affirming that ad visibility does not alter the core functionality promised to the user. The policy must therefore distinguish between what the application actually does today and what it merely might do in a future roadmap. In the current codebase, that means recognizing camera-based scanning, QR generation, local history management, language preferences, optional exporting, advertising or analytics integrations, and the absence of broad account-based processing.

Operational requirements: the developer should document purpose limitation, legal basis where relevant, retention limits, access restrictions, vendor controls, user notice requirements, and escalation paths for exceptions. If a feature is not present, the document should not pretend that it is; instead it should state that the control is reserved for future expansion. That approach is especially valuable for store review, compliance review, and internal audit preparation.

Compliance evidence, version history, and approval records should be retained so the policy can be audited against the shipped build.
