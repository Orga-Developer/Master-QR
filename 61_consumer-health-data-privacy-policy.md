# Consumer Health Data Privacy Policy

This document sets out the governing standard for Consumer Health Data Privacy Policy within the legal architecture of Master QR. Master QR is a camera-first Android application that scans QR codes, generates QR codes, stores scan history locally in Room, supports language switching, provides share/export flows, integrates advertising and analytics components, and is distributed across mobile app stores with platform-specific compliance requirements. The document is written in advanced legal English so it can serve as a publication-ready internal policy, a store-review support artifact, and a future contract annex. It is intentionally precise about scope, permissions, data categories, and release governance, so the text can survive scrutiny across multiple jurisdictions and app ecosystems.

Applicability to Master QR: Where consumer health data laws may apply, this policy should set out the special handling rules for any health-related information the application might process in the future. The current product should avoid health-data collection altogether, but the policy still needs to define collection prohibitions, user notice standards, purpose limits, vendor restrictions, and deletion mechanics if a health feature is ever introduced. This kind of policy is especially useful for demonstrating that the project has a mature governance model even before it reaches sensitive verticals. The policy must therefore distinguish between what the application actually does today and what it merely might do in a future roadmap. In the current codebase, that means recognizing camera-based scanning, QR generation, local history management, language preferences, optional exporting, advertising or analytics integrations, and the absence of broad account-based processing.

Operational requirements: the developer should document purpose limitation, legal basis where relevant, retention limits, access restrictions, vendor controls, user notice requirements, and escalation paths for exceptions. If a feature is not present, the document should not pretend that it is; instead it should state that the control is reserved for future expansion. That approach is especially valuable for store review, compliance review, and internal audit preparation.

Compliance evidence, version history, and approval records should be retained so the policy can be audited against the shipped build.
