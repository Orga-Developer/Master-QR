# Washington My Health My Data Act Compliance Notice

This document sets out the governing standard for Washington My Health My Data Act Compliance Notice within the legal architecture of Master QR. Master QR is a camera-first Android application that scans QR codes, generates QR codes, stores scan history locally in Room, supports language switching, provides share/export flows, integrates advertising and analytics components, and is distributed across mobile app stores with platform-specific compliance requirements. The document is written in advanced legal English so it can serve as a publication-ready internal policy, a store-review support artifact, and a future contract annex. It is intentionally precise about scope, permissions, data categories, and release governance, so the text can survive scrutiny across multiple jurisdictions and app ecosystems.

Applicability to Master QR: This compliance notice is drafted for the possibility that the application may one day encounter consumer health data within scanned or generated content. The notice should affirm that any such data is not deliberately collected in the ordinary QR flow and that the app does not monetize or share it as a business objective. If health data ever enters the product scope, the notice should require meaningful consent, purpose limitation, deletion rights, and clear opt-out mechanics. The current implementation therefore uses the notice as a defensive compliance layer rather than a statement of active health processing. The policy must therefore distinguish between what the application actually does today and what it merely might do in a future roadmap. In the current codebase, that means recognizing camera-based scanning, QR generation, local history management, language preferences, optional exporting, advertising or analytics integrations, and the absence of broad account-based processing.

Operational requirements: the developer should document purpose limitation, legal basis where relevant, retention limits, access restrictions, vendor controls, user notice requirements, and escalation paths for exceptions. If a feature is not present, the document should not pretend that it is; instead it should state that the control is reserved for future expansion. That approach is especially valuable for store review, compliance review, and internal audit preparation.

Compliance evidence, version history, and approval records should be retained so the policy can be audited against the shipped build.
